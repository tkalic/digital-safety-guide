# 01 — Data Privacy

## The Problem Nobody Talks About

Most people think data privacy means "don't share your password." It's a lot more than that.

Every time you use an app, visit a website, or tap "agree" on a terms of service you didn't read, data about you is being collected. Where you are, what you clicked, how long you hesitated before buying something. Individually it seems harmless. Aggregated over years across dozens of platforms, it forms a profile more detailed than most people would be comfortable with.

This guide covers what's actually happening — and what you can practically do about it.

---

## What Gets Collected (And By Whom)

**Platforms and apps** collect behavioral data — what you click, watch, search, and buy. This is used to build advertising profiles and, in some cases, sold to third parties.

**Data brokers** are companies most people have never heard of. They buy, aggregate, and resell personal data from dozens of sources. Your name, address, income estimate, and browsing habits may already be on file somewhere without you ever signing up for anything.

**Your own devices** collect more than you think. Location history, app usage patterns, microphone and camera access — most apps request permissions they don't strictly need.

---

## Key Principles

**1. Data minimization** — The less you share, the less can be exposed. Before filling out any form or signing up for any service, ask: do they actually need this?

**2. Consent means something** — GDPR (EU) and CCPA (California) give you legal rights over your data: the right to access it, correct it, and in many cases delete it. Most people never use these rights because they don't know they exist.

**3. Passwords are the first line of defense** — And most people treat them as an afterthought. Reusing the same password across multiple accounts means one breach exposes everything.

---

## What You Can Do — Practically

### Accounts & Passwords
- Use a password manager (Bitwarden is free and open-source, 1Password is excellent)
- Enable two-factor authentication on every account that offers it — especially email and banking
- Never reuse passwords across different services
- Check [haveibeenpwned.com](https://haveibeenpwned.com) to see if your email has appeared in a data breach

### Browsing
- Use Firefox or Brave as your primary browser — both have stronger default privacy settings than Chrome
- Install uBlock Origin (ad and tracker blocker)
- Use a VPN on public Wi-Fi — your traffic is otherwise readable by anyone on the same network

### Apps & Devices
- Review app permissions regularly — most apps don't need access to your location, contacts, or microphone
- Keep your OS and apps updated — most updates patch security vulnerabilities, not just add features
- Delete accounts and apps you no longer use

---

## Regulations Worth Knowing

**GDPR (EU)** — Requires companies to get explicit consent before collecting your data. Gives you the right to request a copy of your data, correct inaccuracies, and request deletion. Fines for non-compliance can reach 4% of a company's global annual revenue — which is why even US companies take it seriously.

**CCPA (California)** — Gives California residents the right to know what data is collected and opt out of it being sold. Less comprehensive than GDPR but a step in the right direction for US users.

If you're in the EU, you can submit a data access or deletion request to any company that processes your data. They are legally required to respond.
