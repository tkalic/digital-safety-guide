# 05 — Cybersecurity Basics

## Why This Guide Exists

Most digital ethics resources stop at "be responsible online." This one goes a step further — because understanding the actual threats is what makes responsible behavior meaningful rather than just rule-following.

This guide is written from a security student's perspective. Not a threat-actor manual, not a corporate compliance document — a clear explanation of the most common ways things go wrong, and what you can practically do about it.

---

## Passwords — Your First Line of Defense

Most people treat passwords as an afterthought. Attackers count on this.

### How Long Does It Take to Crack a Password?

| Password length | Lowercase only | With numbers | Mixed case + symbols |
|----------------|---------------|-------------|----------------------|
| 8 characters | < 1 second | A few seconds | Minutes |
| 12 characters | A few minutes | Hours | Days |
| 16+ characters | Years | Centuries | Thousands of years |

Length matters more than complexity. `correct-horse-battery-staple` is stronger than `P@ssw0rd` and easier to remember.

### How Attackers Actually Get Your Password

**Brute force** — automated tools try every possible combination. Short passwords fall in seconds.

**Dictionary attacks** — lists of the most common passwords (`password123`, `letmein`, `qwerty`) are tried first. If your password appears on [this list](https://github.com/danielmiessler/SecLists), it will be cracked.

**Credential stuffing** — when a site gets breached, the leaked username/password combinations get tested against every other major service. If you reuse passwords, one breach exposes everything. Check if your email has appeared in a breach: [haveibeenpwned.com](https://haveibeenpwned.com).

**Social engineering** — someone calls your IT helpdesk pretending to be you, or sends a convincing email asking you to reset your password via a link. No technical exploit needed.

### What to Do

- Use a password manager — it generates and stores unique passwords for every account. You only need to remember one master password. [Bitwarden](https://bitwarden.com) is free and open-source. [1Password](https://1password.com) is excellent if you want to pay for it.
- Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on every account that offers it — especially email and banking. An authenticator app (Aegis on Android, Raivo on iOS) is more secure than SMS-based 2FA, which can be intercepted via SIM swapping.
- Hardware keys (YubiKey) are the strongest option for high-value accounts, but overkill for most people.

## Phishing — The Most Common Attack

Phishing is responsible for the majority of successful cyberattacks — not because it's technically sophisticated, but because it works on people, not systems.

### Types of Phishing

**Email phishing** — fake emails impersonating banks, delivery companies, or platforms like PayPal or Microsoft. Usually contain a link to a fake login page.

**Spear phishing** — targeted. The attacker researches you first (LinkedIn, social media) and crafts a message that references real details about your job, colleagues, or recent activity. Much harder to spot.

**Whaling** — spear phishing aimed specifically at executives or high-value individuals.

**Smishing** — phishing via SMS. Common examples: fake delivery notifications, "your account has been locked" messages.

**Vishing** — voice phishing. Someone calls pretending to be from your bank or tech support. They create urgency ("your account has been compromised") and ask for credentials or to install remote access software.

**Quishing** — QR code phishing. Malicious QR codes placed on fake invoices, posters, or even physical stickers over legitimate QR codes (e.g. on EV charging stations or restaurant menus).

**AI-generated phishing** — modern phishing emails are generated by AI and are free of the spelling errors and awkward grammar that used to be the main red flags. Don't rely on poor writing as a signal anymore.

**Deepfake voice and video** — attackers use AI to clone a person's voice or face. A finance employee receives a video call that appears to be from their CEO, authorizing a wire transfer. This has already happened at scale.

### How to Recognize a Phishing Attempt

- Urgency or threats ("your account will be suspended in 24 hours")
- Sender address that looks almost right — `support@paypai.com`, `noreply@amazon-security.net`
- Links that don't match where they claim to go — hover before clicking
- Shortened URLs (`bit.ly`, `tinyurl`) that hide the real destination
- Generic greetings ("Dear customer") rather than your name
- Requests for credentials, payment, or to install software
- Emotional pressure — fear, excitement, or anything that makes you want to act fast without thinking

### What to Do

- Hover over links before clicking. The actual URL is what matters, not the display text.
- When in doubt, go directly to the site by typing the URL — don't use the link in the email.
- Verify urgent requests through a separate channel — call the person back on a number you already have, not one provided in the message.
- Use a separate email address for banking and critical accounts — it reduces your attack surface.
- If you think you've been phished: change your passwords immediately, enable 2FA, run a malware scan (Malwarebytes), contact your bank if financial information was involved.

Take the [Google Phishing Quiz](https://phishingquiz.withgoogle.com/) — it's a quick, practical way to calibrate how well you can spot modern phishing attempts.

## Device Security

### Keep Software Updated

Most successful attacks exploit known vulnerabilities — ones that already have patches available. The reason they still work is that people don't install updates.

Enable automatic updates for your OS, browser, and apps. This single habit eliminates a large percentage of real-world attack vectors.

### Lock Your Devices

- Enable screen lock with a PIN, password, or biometric authentication
- Set auto-lock after a short period of inactivity
- If a device is lost or stolen, use remote wipe: Find My (Apple) or Find My Device (Google)

### App Permissions

Apps routinely request permissions they don't need. Review them regularly — revoke microphone, location, and camera access for any app that doesn't have a clear reason to need it.

Only install apps from official stores (App Store, Google Play). APKs from third-party sources and cracked software are a primary malware delivery vector — and as mentioned in the introduction, the consequences can include legal liability, not just a compromised device.

### Avoid Jailbreaking and Rooting

Jailbroken and rooted devices bypass built-in security mechanisms. They're significantly more vulnerable to malware and unauthorized access. The flexibility isn't worth the exposure for most people.

### Public Wi-Fi

Public Wi-Fi is unencrypted. Anyone on the same network with basic tools can intercept unencrypted traffic. Even with HTTPS being near-universal, other attacks (ARP spoofing, evil twin access points) remain viable.

Use a VPN on public networks. Don't access banking or email on public Wi-Fi without one.

## The Ethics of Security Knowledge

Understanding how attacks work creates responsibility.

The same knowledge that helps you defend a system can be used to attack one. This is why the security community has developed norms around responsible disclosure — if you find a vulnerability, you report it to the affected organization privately before going public, giving them time to fix it. Bug bounty programs formalize this process.

The line between security research and criminal activity is legal and ethical, not technical. Unauthorized access to systems you don't own or have explicit permission to test is illegal in virtually every jurisdiction, regardless of intent.

**CTF (Capture the Flag)** competitions are the legitimate way to develop offensive security skills — intentionally vulnerable systems in controlled environments, built for learning. Good starting points: [TryHackMe](https://tryhackme.com), [HackTheBox](https://hackthebox.com), [PicoCTF](https://picoctf.org).

## Minimum Security Baseline

| Action | Why it matters |
|--------|---------------|
| Unique password per service + password manager | One breach doesn't expose everything |
| 2FA on email and critical accounts | Password alone is not enough |
| OS and app updates installed promptly | Most attacks exploit known, patched vulnerabilities |
| VPN on public Wi-Fi | Prevents traffic interception |
| Hover links before clicking | Defeats most phishing attempts |
| No cracked software or unofficial APKs | Primary malware and legal liability vector |
| App permissions reviewed regularly | Limits data exposure from compromised apps |
| Remote wipe enabled on mobile devices | Protects data if device is lost or stolen |
